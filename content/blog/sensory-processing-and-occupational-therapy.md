# Sensory Processing and Occupational Therapy: What Parents Should Know

Every person processes sensory information differently. Some children are startled by sounds that others barely notice. Some cannot tolerate certain clothing textures. Some seek out intense physical input constantly — crashing into furniture, spinning, or touching everything within reach. These are not simply behavioral problems or discipline issues. They often reflect a genuine difference in how the nervous system processes sensory information.

Occupational therapy is the primary professional discipline that evaluates and addresses sensory processing difficulties in children.

## What Sensory Processing Means

Sensory processing refers to the way the nervous system receives sensory input — from the environment and from within the body — and organizes it into an appropriate response. Most people process sensory information automatically and below the level of conscious awareness. When the processing system works efficiently, a person can attend to what matters, filter out what does not, and respond in ways that are proportionate to the situation.

When sensory processing is disrupted, the result can look very different in different children. Some children are hypersensitive — they overreact to sensory input that others barely notice. Light touch may feel painful. Certain food textures may trigger gagging. Specific sounds may cause intense distress. These children often appear to be behaviorally difficult when they are actually experiencing genuine physical discomfort.

Other children are hyposensitive — they underrespond to sensory input and seem to need more of it than usual. These children may seek out intense movement, crash into things, touch everything, or appear not to notice pain.

Many children show a mix of both patterns — oversensitive in some sensory areas and undersensitive in others.

## The Sensory Systems Involved

When people think of sensory processing, they typically think of the five classic senses. But occupational therapists work with two additional sensory systems that are equally important:

**Tactile (touch):** Sensitivity to textures on the skin, clothing tags, light touch, and temperature. Children with tactile sensitivity may resist certain clothing, avoid messy play, or react intensely to being touched unexpectedly.

**Auditory (sound):** Sensitivity to volume, pitch, and background noise. Children who are sound-sensitive may cover their ears frequently, become dysregulated in noisy environments, or have difficulty concentrating when there is background noise.

**Visual:** Sensitivity to bright lights, busy visual environments, or certain colors and patterns.

**Gustatory and olfactory (taste and smell):** Strong reactions to food tastes, textures, and smells that affect eating and participation in meals.

**Vestibular:** The sense of movement and balance, processed in the inner ear. Children with vestibular processing differences may fear heights or movement, become easily motion sick, or conversely seek out constant spinning and swinging.

**Proprioception:** The sense of where the body is in space and how much force is being exerted, sensed through muscles and joints. Children with proprioceptive processing differences often seek out heavy work — pushing, pulling, carrying, crashing — to satisfy their nervous system.

**Interoception:** The sense of what is happening inside the body — hunger, thirst, the need to use the bathroom, heart rate, temperature. Difficulty with interoception can affect self-regulation and emotional awareness.

## How Sensory Processing Difficulties Affect Daily Life

Sensory processing differences affect many areas of daily life:

- **Mealtimes:** Food texture, temperature, and smell sensitivities can make mealtimes a significant source of stress for the whole family
- **Dressing:** Clothing textures, tags, seams, and tightness can cause genuine discomfort
- **Grooming:** Haircuts, tooth brushing, and nail trimming may be experienced as painful or threatening
- **School:** Noisy cafeterias, transitions between activities, and the sensory demands of the classroom can overwhelm a child's regulatory capacity
- **Social participation:** Sensory overload in group settings can contribute to behavioral outbursts and social withdrawal
- **Sleep:** Sensory sensitivity can make it difficult to settle and stay asleep

## How Occupational Therapy Helps

Occupational therapists use a structured, evidence-informed approach to address sensory processing difficulties. A sensory evaluation identifies the specific patterns of sensitivity and seeks across the different sensory systems. From this evaluation, the OT develops an individualized treatment approach.

**Sensory integration therapy** is a specialized OT approach that uses structured, purposeful sensory activities to help the nervous system learn to process sensory input more effectively. Sessions are typically conducted in a sensory gym equipped with swings, therapy balls, tactile materials, and other equipment designed to provide controlled sensory input.

**The sensory diet** is an individualized plan of sensory activities and environmental modifications that a child can use throughout the day to maintain optimal alertness and regulation. Parents are taught to implement the sensory diet at home.

**Environmental modifications** address the sensory demands of the child's environment — adjusting lighting, sound levels, clothing choices, and classroom seating to reduce sensory overload.

The goal is not to eliminate sensory sensitivity but to help the child's nervous system process sensory input more effectively and to give the child and their family tools for managing sensory challenges in daily life.

## Getting an Evaluation

If you suspect your child has sensory processing difficulties, a referral to an occupational therapist for a sensory evaluation is the appropriate first step. Look specifically for an OT with training and experience in sensory processing and sensory integration. A thorough evaluation will give you clear information about your child's sensory profile and a roadmap for how occupational therapy can help.
